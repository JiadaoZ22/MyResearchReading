Abstract
Alzheimer’s disease is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder that is challenging to diagnose at an early stage. Affecting 
over 55 million people worldwide, its prevalence is expected to rise sharply by 2030. The use of artificial intelligence (AI) 
techniques has become increasingly important to improve the speed and accuracy of diagnosis. In this study, we propose the 
NCA-Enhanced Voting Algorithm for Alzheimer’s Classification (NCA-EVA) to support computer-aided diagnosis. A total 
of 66 models were trained for four-class data and six models for two-class data. The proposed method successfully classified 
all four stages of Alzheimer’s disease, achieving 98.97% accuracy in four-class classification and 99.87% accuracy in binary 
classification. Moreover, with a processing time of just 1.26 s, NCA-EVA is approximately 1200 times faster than a compara-
ble study using NCA-based feature selection. These findings demonstrate that Alzheimer’s diagnosis can be performed both 
quickly and with high accuracy, and the proposed approach has potential applications in other healthcare data analysis tasks.

Keywords  Alzheimer’s disease · Artificial intelligence · Vision Transformers · CNN · Feature extraction

Introduction

Background

Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is a neurodegenerative disorder 
that results in cognitive decline in the brain. This disease 
causes the decline of cognitive functions and progressively 
worsens over time. This trend results in a decline in cogni-
tive function throughout the disease [1]. Alzheimer’s disease 
is characterized as a progressive condition with five primary 
stages [2]. This disease is challenging to identify in everyday 
life as it does not exhibit conspicuous signs until it reaches 
severe stages. If not recognized promptly, the condition will 
advance and cause harm to the brain.

Currently,  there  is  no  medication  available  to  halt  or 
reverse Alzheimer’s disease. The therapy approach does not 
offer a cure for the condition, and the timing and causes of 
this chronic degenerative process remain mostly unknown.

Diagnosis  of  this  condition  is  challenging  until  severe 
symptoms are evident, and it is believed to be influenced by 
environmental or genetic factors, including previous head 
trauma, hypertension, and depression [3]. The 2021 Alz-
heimer’s Disease International (ADI) report indicates that a 
survey was administered to 1111 multidisciplinary doctors, 
205 dementia patients, and 2122 dementia caregivers. This 
poll indicates that 47% refrain from seeking a diagnosis due 
to insufficient access to qualified specialists, 34% due to the 
financial burden of diagnosis and treatment, and 46% due 
to apprehension about a dementia diagnosis. These rates 
have been recognized as substantial impediments to diag-
nosis. Additional obstacles to clinical diagnoses encompass 
restricted access to specialist diagnostic tests (38%) and the 
perception among patients and their families that no inter-
ventions are possible following a dementia diagnosis (33%) 
[4]. Given these percentages, the early diagnostic rate of 
Alzheimer’s disease is deemed highly challenging.

Globally, 55 million individuals have received a diagnosis 
of Alzheimer’s disease. This figure is anticipated to rise dra-
matically by 2030. Given the clinical, social, and economic 
circumstances, early diagnosis is significantly challenging. 
Given the numerous illness outbreaks globally and the prev-
alent skepticism towards treatment protocols, the rising inci-
dence of Alzheimer’s disease is accompanied by challenges

in diagnostic and therapeutic intervention. Furthermore, the 
ADI report released in 2021 indicated that almost 30% of 
fatalities ascribed to Alzheimer’s disease were inaccurately 
diagnosed. Re-evaluations with computerized imaging scans 
were performed on these patients, yielding a 25% correction 
in diagnosis. Given this situation, the utilization of magnetic

Alzheimer’s disease, is a significant measure in mitigating

Alzheimer’s disease is a disorder that impairs cognitive 
skills due to neurodegenerative processes. The aggregation 
of beta-amyloid plaques and tau protein tangles constitutes 
the basic foundation of the disease. These aggregates impede 
neural communication, causing synapse loss and ultimately 
culminating  in  neurodegeneration  marked  by  neuronal 
death [5]. These biological processes predominantly influ-
ence brain regions associated with memory and cognitive 
functioning, including the hippocampus and cortex. The 
demise of neuronal cells and synaptic degradation results in 
significant deficits in memory, language, problem-solving, 
and daily functioning.

The initial phases of Alzheimer’s disease are character-
ized by symptom ambiguity and their conflation with aging 
indications, which complicates diagnosis. Early diagnosis 
is  essential  for  the  prompt  commencement  of  therapies 
designed to mitigate disease development [6]. Nonetheless, 
the existing diagnostic approaches lack adequate precision 
at this stage. Imaging modalities like MRI and CT scans are 
essential instruments for identifying structural alterations 
in the brain; however, these changes generally manifest in 
the advanced stages of the disease. Advanced technologies, 
including biomarker analysis and PET scans for early diag-
nosis, provide enhanced accuracy; however, their extensive 
clinical application is restricted by cost and accessibility 
limitations [7].

AI-assisted diagnostic methods represent a significant 
advancement for the early detection of Alzheimer’s disease. 
AI systems can scrutinize extensive datasets to identify 
nuanced alterations in brain pictures, facilitating the early 
identification of the condition. These devices facilitate the 
expedited and precise assessment of MRI and CT imaging 
outcomes, specifically [8]. AI-driven decision support sys-
tems minimize inaccuracies in clinical diagnostic procedures 
and enhance treatment administration. AI-based decision 
support systems enhance treatment management and lower 
errors in clinical diagnosis procedures. The growing appli-
cation of AI in healthcare has novel prospects for the early 
diagnosis and therapy of neurodegenerative disorders such 
as Alzheimer’s [9].

In the advanced stages of Alzheimer’s disease, alterations 
in brain structure become increasingly evident, but in the

initial stages, these changes are sometimes imperceptible or 
indistinguishable. Consequently, in addition to conventional 
imaging techniques, sophisticated computer-aided diagnos-

have not yet manifested as clinical symptoms through the

Motivation

Deep learning architectures and multimodal data fusion 
approaches have significantly propelled recent improve-
ments in the diagnosis of Alzheimer’s. Numerous studies in 
the literature indicate that these methods provide substantial 
advantages in enhancing diagnostic precision and stream-
lining processing durations. Prasath and Sumathi devised a 
deep learning technique utilizing the Pipelined LeNet (PLN) 
architecture for the early detection of Alzheimer’s disease. 
This  approach  employed  an  image  fusion  technique  to 
improve the interior areas of low-resolution MRI images. It 
differentiated between Alzheimer’s and healthy brain images 
with 99.5% accuracy utilizing a PLN architecture including 
six convolutional layers and six sampling layers [12].

Qiao et al. have created a deep learning model named 
Convolutional-Squeeze-Excitation-Softmax-NET (CSES-
NET). This model categorizes Alzheimer’s disease, early 
mild  cognitive  impairment  (EMCI),  late  mild  cognitive 
impairment (LMCI), and healthy controls by synthesiz-
ing multi-channel data, including voxel-based morphom-
etry, surface morphometry, and radiometric characteristics 
derived from MRI data. The CSES-NET model has exhib-
ited exceptional performance, achieving an accuracy rate of 
96.17%, and has analyzed the correlations between genetic 
and radiomic aspects of Alzheimer’s disease [13].

Lu et al. have implemented a methodology that amalgam-
ates MRI, genetic, and clinical data for Alzheimer’s diagno-
sis through the creation of the Hierarchical Attention-Based 
Multi-Modal Fusion (HAMF) model. This model has been 
highly effective in assessing the risk of progression from 
mild cognitive impairment (MCI) to Alzheimer’s disease 
and has emerged as a viable instrument for the early diag-
nosis of Alzheimer’s, with an accuracy rate of 87.2% [14].

Sasikala et al. created a CAD system that uses MRI data 
to diagnose Alzheimer’s disease in their published papers 
from 2024. Structures like gray matter (GM), white matter 

 
Journal of Imaging Informatics in Medicine 

(WM), and cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) were correctly identi-
fied during brain tissue segmentation using the iteratively 
reweighted adaptive spatial fuzzy c-means (IRW-ASFCM) 
approach. Features were extracted using a 2LS-WaveCNN 
ensemble, which is a two-level selective wavelet kernel con-
volutional neural network. After that, the neural network lay-
ers were trained using the deep tree training (DTT) method. 
The  specificity  rate  was  99.64%;  the  accuracy  rate  was 
99.26%, and the precision rate was 99.26% among the Alz-
heimer’s, cognitively normal (CN), and MCI classes, respec-
tively. The investigation of the proposed model’s processing 
time also showed that it outperforms previous models in 
terms of performance and time complexity; the model took 
97.88 s to finish the whole processing [15].

In their 2024 paper, Zhao et al. performed an exhaustive 
analysis of AI applications for Alzheimer’s disease. This 
study addresses the application of AI in etiology discovery, 
computer-aided diagnostic (CAD), computer-aided progno-
sis (CAP), and treatment domains. The objective has been to 
enhance the diagnosis and prognosis of Alzheimer’s disease 
by the utilization of multi-modal data, including genetic, 
neuroimaging, and linguistic information. The research has 
attained application rates of 65% in CAD systems, 11% in 
prognostic studies, 19% in biomarker identification, and 5% 
in treatment utilizing AI. Zhao and his associates asserted 
that AI methodologies may enhance clinical decision-mak-
ing by offering efficient solutions for disease detection and 
therapy [16].

In a 2024 study, Sorour et al. introduced a deep learning 
(DL) methodology utilizing MRI data for the early diag-
nosis of AD. The study employed five distinct deep learn-
ing models: CNNs without augmentation, CNNs with aug-
mentation, CNNs combined with LSTM and augmentation, 
CNNs integrated with SVM and augmentation, and VGG16 
combined with SVM and augmentation. These models were 
autonomously trained for feature extraction and classifica-
tion tasks. The models are categorized based on the applica-
tion of data augmentation, with particular emphasis on the 
VGG16-SVM model, which is enhanced by transfer learning 
techniques. The testing findings demonstrated that the CNN-
LSTM model achieved an accuracy of 99.92%, indicating 
improved performance [17].

Huang  et  al.  devised  a  novel  approach  in  their  2024 
research, integrating the Deep Forest algorithm with near-
infrared (NIR) spectroscopy for the diagnosis of AD and 
vascular dementia (VaD). This study evaluated serum sam-
ples from the AD and VaD groups utilizing the aquaphoto-
mics technique, which investigates the influence of water 
molecules on hydrogen bonding. The Deep Forest model has 
surpassed typical machine learning techniques, attaining an 
accuracy of 98.67%, sensitivity of 97.33%, and specificity of 
100%. This approach integrates the rapidity and precision of 
NIR spectroscopy with the robust modeling proficiency of 

deep learning, facilitating the swift and accurate distinction 
between AD and VaD [18].

Rezaee and Zhu, in their 2024 work, devised a deep learn-
ing model that combines CascadeNet with manually con-
structed characteristics to identify Alzheimer’s disease (AD) 
and mild cognitive impairment (MCI) from EEG waves. In 
the study, EEG signals were initially divided into subbands 
by discrete wavelet transform (DWT), and then, data aug-
mentation was executed via the windowing method. The 
enhanced CascadeNet model was subsequently employed 
for feature extraction and classification tasks involving these 
signals. This model has demonstrated efficacy in detecting 
Alzheimer’s disease (AD) and mild cognitive impairment 
(MCI), particularly with limited datasets, by attaining high 
accuracy in spectral pictures derived from EEG signals. 
The fine-tuning and training duration for each CNN block 
of the Cascade-ResNet-18 model is designated as 4 to 6 h. 
The research attained accuracy rates of 98.84% and 97.78% 
on the Figshare and Brazil datasets, respectively, indicating 
enhanced performance relative to current methodologies 
[19].

Ayus and Gupta devised a hybrid model named Hybrid 
Recurrent Ensemble Network (HReENet), employing deep 
learning methodologies for the diagnosis of Alzheimer’s 
disease in their 2024 article. This model seeks to categorize 
the four stages of Alzheimer’s disease (NonDemented, Very-
MildlyDemented, MildlyDemented, ModeratelyDemented) 
by integrating various deep learning architectures, includ-
ing CNN, Conv1D-LSTM, and LSTM. The model utilized 
the  Kaggle  Alzheimer’s  classification  dataset,  attaining 
an accuracy of 99.97%, a precision of 99.98%, a recall of 
99.95%, and an F1-score of 99.97%. The suggested model 
has exhibited enhanced performance relative to previous 
models, including CNN and LSTM, markedly improving 
classification accuracy [20].

Rani et al. developed an optimal clustering-based pre-
trained SqueezeNet model for the early diagnosis of Alz-
heimer’s disease in their study. The research employed the 
Adaptive Reptile Search Algorithm (ARSA) for the seg-
mentation of gray matter (GM), white matter (WM), and 
cerebrospinal fluid (CSF). ARSA enhances segmentation 
accuracy by refining the identification of cluster centers. The 
pre-trained SqueezeNet model was subsequently employed 
to classify various phases of Alzheimer’s disease. The exper-
imental studies of the ADNI and OASIS datasets indicated 
that the model attained accuracy rates over 98%. The results 
demonstrate that the model provides an efficient approach 
for diagnosing Alzheimer’s disease [21].

Alp  et  al.  developed  a  method  that  combines  Vision 
Transformer  (ViT)  and  Time  Series  Transformer  (TST) 
architectures to identify Alzheimer’s disease (AD) [22]. To 
solve the problems encountered during the processing of 
3D MRI data, the images were divided into two different 

three-dimensional slices, and transfer learning techniques 
were used. The ADNI dataset was used for binary and mul-
ticlass classifications. The results showed that the method 
demonstrated superior performance with 99.048% accuracy 
in binary classification and 99.014% accuracy in multiclass 
classification. These findings highlight the effectiveness of 
sequence modeling methods and self-attention mechanisms 
in medical image analysis, providing a powerful solution 
for the early detection and classification of Alzheimer’s dis-
ease. Literature summary of the reviewed studies is given 
in Table 1.

Recent studies have shown that deep learning-based 
approaches achieve very high accuracy rates in the diag-
nosis  of  Alzheimer’s  disease  [12– 22].  However,  these 
methods have significant limitations. Many studies focus 
solely  on  binary  classification  and  are  insufficient  in 

Journal of Imaging Informatics in Medicine

simultaneously detecting the different stages of Alzhei-
mer’s disease (No Dementia, Very Mild Dementia, Mild 
Dementia, Moderate Dementia) with high accuracy. In 
addition, many models in the literature have a long pro-
cessing time, making it difficult to use them as real-time 
decision support systems in clinical settings. Some meth-
ods focus only on a single data modality (e.g., MRI only), 
neglecting the combined use of different feature extraction 
techniques and dimension reduction methods. Consider-
ing these shortcomings, there is a need for a new method 
that enables early and accurate diagnosis of Alzheimer’s 
disease, while also offering high performance on multi-
class data and operating quickly. Motivated by this, the 
NCA-EVA method proposed in our study offers significant 
improvements in both accuracy and processing time com-
pared to existing approaches in the literature for multi-
stage Alzheimer’s classification.

Material and Method

Dataset

Alzheimer’s  disease  is  a  neurological  disorder  that  is 
becoming more prevalent due to the aging global popula-
tion, resulting in significant repercussions such as memory 
impairment and cognitive decline. The accurate diagno-
sis of the disease and its stage classification are crucial 
for comprehending its progression and for implementing 
suitable therapy and intervention strategies. Consequently, 
brain images acquired by magnetic resonance imaging 
(MRI) techniques serve as a significant data source for 
identifying various phases of Alzheimer’s disease. The 
dataset included in this research comprises images depict-
ing four distinct stages of Alzheimer’s disease, systemati-
cally classified and balanced to categorize the disease’s 
symptoms [23]. The dataset was obtained from the pub-
licly available Kaggle platform, where it is shared under 
an open data usage license. Therefore, no ethical approval 
was required for its use in this study.

The Alzheimer’s multi-class dataset is an open-source 
collection of MRI scans depicting various phases of Alzhei-
mer’s disease for diagnostic and classification purposes. This 
multi-class dataset comprises around 44,000 MRI scans, 
classified into four distinct categories based on the severity 
of Alzheimer’s disease. This dataset, designed for training 
and evaluating machine learning models, comprises MRI 
images exclusively featuring brain tissue devoid of any non-
cranial tissue components. Sample images of Alzheimer’s 
stages contained in the dataset are shown in Fig. 1.

The dataset comprises four primary files, each corre-
sponding to a distinct severity class of Alzheimer’s disease. 
Each folder comprises images corresponding to a particular 
stage of the disease: NonDemented (12,800 data), VeryMild-
Demented (11,200 data), MildDemented (10,000 data), and 
ModerateDemented (10,000 data). NonDemented persons 
exhibit no indications of dementia. VeryMildDemented has 
a few symptoms and is in the preliminary phase of the con-
dition. Mild dementia presents notable yet still moderate 
symptoms of cognitive decline. Individuals with moder-
ate dementia have more intense and intermediate signs of 

the condition. All images in this collection are depicted as 
“skull-stripped,” indicating that extraneous tissue and bone 
features outside the cranial region have been eliminated from 
the images. Exclusively incorporating brain tissue facilitates 
model concentration and mitigates the impact of extraneous 
information. The dimensions and resolution of the images 
have probably been adjusted to ensure they are sufficiently 
large for deep-learning models to analyze. Standardizing 
MRI images facilitates data processing and integration into 
the model.

Feature Extraction with Vision Transformers (ViTs) 
and EfficientNet

In recent years, deep learning-based feature extraction meth-
ods have significantly advanced medical image analysis and 
have become essential in disease detection. Feature extrac-
tion denotes the automated identification and extraction of 
salient and significant characteristics from photographs. This 
procedure is particularly crucial in the evaluation of MRI 
images utilized in diagnosing neurodegenerative disorders, 
including Alzheimer’s disease. Deep learning models’ fea-
ture extraction capabilities offer a more thorough and accu-
rate analysis than older methods, facilitating more efficient 
identification of disease-specific visual biomarkers. In this 
context, EfficientNet and ViT architectures illustrate two dif-
ferent techniques for feature extraction.

The EfficientNet architecture is a novel methodology for 
deep learning, providing an innovative perspective on image 
classification and feature extraction tasks. This architecture 
enhances feature extraction efficiency compared to classic 
models by employing a compound scaling strategy that opti-
mally balances the network’s depth, width, and resolution. 
MBConv (Mobile Inverted Bottleneck Convolution) blocks, 
the fundamental component of EfficientNet, operate along-
side  pointwise  convolution,  depthwise  convolution,  and 
Squeeze-and-Excitation modules to effectively capture both 
low-level and high-level image characteristics. This feature 
extraction method is proficient in assessing intricate visual 
structures and identifying significant landmarks.

The feature extraction capabilities of EfficientNet arise 
from the architecture’s novel topology. The composite scal-
ing approach methodically adjusts the three essential dimen-
sions of the model architecture (depth, width, and resolution), 
enhancing the efficacy of each dimension’s contribution. In 
contrast with traditional convolutional neural networks (CNN), 
this method architecture preserves computational efficiency 
while augmenting model size and generating more effective 
feature representations. MBConv blocks effectively capture 
both local and global information due to their bottleneck archi-
tecture and inverted residual connections. Squeeze-and-Exci-
tation modules provide channel-wise feature calibration, ena-
bling the amplification of significant features while attenuating 

Journal of Imaging Informatics in Medicine

trivial ones. The amalgamation of these architectural elements 
renders EfficientNet an exceptionally proficient model for fea-
ture extraction.

Vision Transformers employ a transformer-based design 
instead of CNNs for computer vision tasks, where images are 
divided into small patches and processed sequentially through 
transformer layers [24]. A 224 × 224 pixel image is segmented 
into 196 patches of 16 × 16 pixels, and positional data are 
added to help the model understand the spatial relationships 
between patches [25].

The primary element of transformers is the multi-head self-
attention mechanism [26]. This method acquires significant 
information by focusing on various areas of the image. These 
layers ascertain the interrelations among image segments and 
progressively refine the derived attributes. 

The ViT models employed in the study vary in size, each 
optimized for a particular resolution and dimension (Fig. 2). 
The study employs two distinct ViT architectures: ViT Base 
16/32 and ViT Large 16/32. ViT Base is one of the funda-
mental iterations within the ViT family. This model segments 
the input image into small blocks of 16 × 16 and 32 × 32 and 
processes each block through transformer layers. This model 
possesses limited parameters and is efficient for small to 
medium-sized datasets. It is mainly utilized in classification 
tasks that require high precision but do not demand extensive 
datasets. The ViT Large model is an expanded version of the 
model. It is optimized for extensive datasets and incorporates 
additional parameters.

Feature selection is an essential procedure for enhancing the 
efficacy of machine learning models and reducing computing 
complexity. This study employs three distinct feature selec-
tion methods to efficiently identify high-dimensional features 
extracted from brain MRI images and execute dimensionality 
reduction. ElasticNet (ENet), NCA, and MRMR algorithms 
each seek to uncover the most discriminative features in the 
dataset through distinct mathematical methodologies and opti-
mization strategies. This section will study in detail the theo-
retical underpinnings, operational principles, and significance 
of feature selection algorithms in the context of Alzheimer’s 
categorization.

ENet  is  an  efficient  technique  for  diminishing  the

such as medical image analysis.

NCA is a supervised dimensionality reduction technique 
that improves classification performance by refining the 
distance metrics among data items [28]. The algorithm’s 
primary goal is to build a transformation matrix that maps 
data into a lower-dimensional space while preserving class 
associations [29].

In its mathematical framework, for a dataset, X ∈ Rn × d, 
NCA learns a linear transformation matrix A ∈ Rk × d that 
maps data points from d-dimensional space to k-dimensional 
space through yi = Axi. The probability of point i choosing 
point j as a neighbor is defined as pij ∝ e^(− ∥yi − yj∥2). This 
transformation maximizes the likelihood of correct classifi-
cation based on neighborhood relationships.

NCA’s  effectiveness  stems  from  its  ability  to  han-
dle  high-dimensional  data  while  preserving  essential 

class-specific  features.  It  finds  applications  in  various 
domains, including image recognition, natural language 
processing,  and  biological  data  analysis.  The  method 
enhances feature representation by bringing same-class 
data points closer while separating different classes [28, 
29]. This approach particularly benefits distance-based 
classification methods by creating an optimal framework 
for accurate categorization, making it especially valuable 
for complex datasets like medical imaging analysis.

Algorithms for Classifications

In this study, the performance of different machine learn-
ing algorithms for the classification of Alzheimer’s disease 
is comparatively analyzed. Six distinct classification algo-
rithms—support vector machine (SVM), gradient boosting 
machine (GBM), XGBoost, random forest (RF), logistic 
regression (LR), and k-nearest neighbors (KNN)—possess 
unique mathematical underpinnings and operational prin-

The other classifier model is the LR model. LR is a linear 
model created to address classification issues, extensively 
utilized in statistics and machine learning [36]. Logistic 
regression is employed to forecast the likelihood of an event 
transpiring and is especially effective in binary classifica-
tion challenges. Notwithstanding the term “regression” in 
its designation, its objective is to categorize data and predict 
the class to which the data pertains rather than to estimate 
continuous values. Logistic regression is appropriate for cat-
egorization, in contrast to linear regression. Linear regres-
sion forecasts the dependent variable as a continuous value; 
nevertheless, this method is inappropriate for classification 
problems, as the dependent variable is typically categorical 
or binary. Logistic regression addresses this issue by con-
structing a model from linear combinations of independent 
variables and using a sigmoid function to convert the output 
of a linear equation into a constrained range (between 0 and 
1) [37]. This function generates numbers that can be read as 
estimated probabilities, which are classified into categories 
based on a specific threshold value.

The sigmoid function is fundamental to logistic regres-
sion. The sigmoid function generates classification predic-
tions by constraining the model’s output within the range of 
0 to 1 [38]. The sigmoid function is shown in Eq. 1.

combination into a number ranging from 0 to 1. If the output 
value exceeds 0.5, the model categorizes the input as one

Logistic regression, while primarily utilized for binary 
classification, can also be employed for multiclass classifica-
tion through methods such as one-vs-rest or softmax regres-
sion. In the one-vs-rest methodology, each class is forecasted 
in relation to the other classes using an individual binary 
classification model. Softmax regression, conversely, iden-
tifies the class with the highest probability by concurrently 
calculating the probabilities for each class in multiclass clas-
sifications. Logistic regression yields highly effective out-
comes, particularly with linearly separable datasets. Logis-
tic regression is both efficient and precise when the dataset 
does not necessitate intricate border delineations for accurate 
categorization. It is distinguished as an efficient classifica-
tion model due to its lower processing demands compared 
to more intricate methods. Logistic regression may exhibit 
constrained efficacy on intricate and non-linear classification 
problems due to its reliance on linear bounds; yet, it serves 
as a robust model when linear precision is essential, estab-
lishing a decision boundary suitable for the data.

GBM is an effective machine learning system that mini-
mizes  error  rates  and  enhances  prediction  accuracy  by 
sequentially aggregating weak learners, typically decision 
trees. Using a boosting strategy, GBM adds a new model 
at each iteration by learning the faults  of  the preceding 
model [39, 40]. During this procedure, model parameters 
are refined by gradient descent, therefore minimizing the 
overall error [41].

The efficacy of GBM is contingent upon the precise cali-
bration of parameters, including learning rate, tree quantity, 
and depth. Each additional tree incrementally enhances the 
model’s accuracy by assimilating the residual mistakes from 
prior iterations. The algorithm proficiently comprehends 
intricate data patterns and methodically minimizes errors, 
particularly in high-dimensional datasets. Its proficiency in 
managing data noise and superior generalization capabili-
ties render it an exceptionally beneficial option for intricate 
classification challenges.

XGBoost is an enhanced iteration of the gradient boost-
ing method, tailored for challenges necessitating superior 
performance and accuracy [42]. Regularized learning and 
gradient descent techniques define the approach. It has fea-
tures including L1 and L2 regularization, parallel processing 
capabilities, out-of-memory computation for extensive data-
sets, and management of missing data. Using advanced tree 
pruning mechanisms and growth algorithms, XGBoost [43] 
minimizes needless complexity while maintaining appropri-
ate tree depth.

The algorithm’s success is contingent upon precisely 
calibrating parameters like learning rate, maximum depth, 

number of trees, and sampling rate. It is distinguished by its 
capacity to proficiently identify data linkages and handle 
missing data, particularly in intricate datasets. These attrib-
utes render XGBoost a more advantageous choice compared 
to conventional boosting algorithms.

KNN is a supervised learning technique that classifies 
data points based on their proximity to neighboring ones 
[44]. The approach computes the distance of the data point 
to be classified from other instances in the dataset and pre-
dicts by assigning the majority class of the k-nearest neigh-
bors. The efficacy of KNN is contingent upon the selection 
of k and the employed distance metric (Euclidean, Manhat-
tan, or Minkowski). In high-dimensional areas, algorithm 
efficiency may diminish due to the “curse of dimensional-
ity,” which can be mitigated using dimensionality reduction 
techniques like PCA. Despite being a nonparametric and 
straightforward model, it is computationally demanding with 
large datasets. It is referred to as a “lazy learner” because it 
conducts categorization during prediction instead of learning 
during training. The efficacy of the model is contingent upon 
data preprocessing and the caliber of the training data, with 
performance enhancement achievable through approaches 
like normalization.

Proposed Method

This work presents the NCA-Enhanced Voting Algorithm 
for Alzheimer’s Classification (NCA-EVA), which intro-
duces  a  novel  hybrid  methodology  that  enhances  fea-
ture selection and classification techniques (Fig. 3). This 
method is intended to effectively process and categorize 
features obtained from any feature extraction methodology 
and has two primary stages. Initially, the NCA method is 
employed to identify the most distinguishing features from 
the retrieved data. In the second stage, a Voting Classifier 
comprising KNN, SVM, and RF algorithms is utilized to 
categorize the chosen features. This method integrates the 
feature selection efficacy of NCA with the classification 
strength of the ensemble learning model to enhance clas-
sification performance and optimize processing duration.

The choice of algorithms employed in the NCA-EVA 
technique was predicated on the strengths and benefits of 
each algorithm. NCA is a feature selection approach tailored 
for classification tasks, utilized during the feature selection 
step. The primary rationale for selecting NCA is its capacity 
to learn the distance metric among features by allocating a 
weight to each feature. Thus, it can discern the characteris-
tics that most effectively differentiate between classes and 
execute dimensionality reduction proficiently. Furthermore, 
in the experiments, the NCA algorithm achieved the most 
favorable outcomes among several feature selection algo-
rithms, resulting in its preference for the proposed strategy.

Experimental Results

This paper presents the results of several experiments and 
the proposed NCA-EVA method to improve the classifica-
tion performance of Alzheimer’s disease. The studies are 
discussed in two parts. In the first part, 66 models are trained 
on four-class data. Then, the NCA-EVA method is applied 
to 6 models with two-class data, and results are obtained 
for comparison with two-class studies in the literature. To 
ensure the robustness and reliability of the proposed method, 
a fivefold cross-validation strategy was applied. The data-
set was randomly partitioned into five equal subsets, where 
four folds were used for training and one fold for testing in 
each iteration. This process was repeated five times, and the 
final performance metrics were reported as the average of 
these iterations. The dataset used in this study is balanced 
across the four classes, thereby minimizing the risk of class 
imbalance. Additionally, the use of cross-validation helped 
reduce the risk of overfitting and ensured that the reported 
results accurately reflect the generalization performance of 
the model.

Conclusion and Discussion

In  this  study,  we  proposed  NCA-EVA,  an  approach 
designed to classify different stages of Alzheimer’s dis-
ease with high accuracy and exceptionally short process-
ing time. The method effectively addresses several key 
limitations of existing approaches in multi-stage Alzhei-
mer classification. Using a four-class dataset, our model 
achieved  an  accuracy  of  98.97%  in  distinguishing  No 
Dementia, Very Mild Dementia, Mild Dementia, and Mod-
erate Dementia. When reduced to a two-class problem, the 
accuracy further increased to 99.87%, demonstrating the 
robustness and adaptability of the proposed framework. 
Moreover, NCA-EVA achieved these results with a pro-
cessing time that was approximately 1200 times faster than 
traditional NCA-based methods, completing inference in 
just 1.26 s. This computational efficiency highlights its 
potential for real-time clinical deployment as a decision 
support system.

When compared with previous studies, our results repre-
sent a significant advancement. For instance, Sasikala et al. 
[15] reported an accuracy of 99.26% for a binary classifica-
tion problem using a deep CNN. In contrast, our method 
achieved 99.87%, offering a slight but consistent improve-
ment with far greater processing speed. Other ViT-based 
studies, such as Xin et al. [18] and Soundarya et al. [19], 
reported accuracy rates in the range of 90–96% for multi-
class Alzheimer classification. In contrast, our four-class 
model reached 98.97%, surpassing these benchmarks. This 
demonstrates that combining advanced feature extraction 
techniques with the hybrid NCA-EVA strategy provides a 
clear performance benefit over single-model approaches. 
The improvement is not only numerical but also methodo-
logical, as our method integrates the strengths of multiple 
classifiers while drastically reducing computational costs.

The clinical relevance of these findings is substantial. 
Alzheimer’s disease diagnosis is often time-consuming and 
relies heavily on expert interpretation of MRI scans. The 
ability of NCA-EVA to deliver near-instant, highly accu-
rate predictions can support neurologists and neuroradiolo-
gists by reducing diagnostic workload and enabling earlier 
interventions. For example, in busy hospital environments, 
the model could serve as a real-time triage tool, quickly 
identifying cases requiring urgent follow-up. Furthermore, 
the extremely short runtime ensures that the method can be 
integrated into clinical workflows without adding delays, 
which is particularly important for screening programs and 
resource-limited healthcare settings. These advantages high-
light the translational potential of our work from computa-
tional research to real-world medical applications.

Despite  these  promising  results,  several  limitations 
should be acknowledged. First, the dataset used in this 

study consists of 44,000 MRI slices, which represent mul-
tiple images from a smaller group of patients rather than 
44,000 unique individuals. Although the data provider 
prepared the dataset in a pre-balanced and pre-shuffled 
format to minimize overlap, there remains a possibility 
of correlation between slices from the same patient. This 
could introduce bias if images from one patient were inad-
vertently distributed across training, validation, and test 
sets. While this risk is expected to have minimal effect due 
to the balanced nature of the dataset and the comparative 
focus of this study, it remains a methodological limitation. 
Future studies will explicitly apply patient-level splitting 
to ensure generalizability further and reduce potential bias.
Second,  this  study  did  not  utilize  a  real-world,  inde-
pendent external dataset for validation. While the proposed 
method demonstrates exceptional performance on the cur-
rent dataset, independent validation using diverse, real-world 
clinical data is essential before clinical deployment. Addi-
tionally, no direct collaboration with clinicians or neurora-
diologists was conducted during this study, which will be 
addressed in future work by incorporating expert feedback 
and clinical test scenarios.

In  conclusion,  the  NCA-EVA  method  emerges  as  a 
highly accurate, efficient, and clinically relevant solution 
for the early detection and staging of Alzheimer’s disease. 
Compared to conventional machine learning algorithms 
and existing deep learning models, it provides a substan-
tial reduction in processing time and sets a new benchmark 
for multi-class Alzheimer classification. By addressing the 
limitations noted above—particularly through patient-level 
data handling and external validation—this approach has the 
potential to be integrated into clinical diagnostic pipelines. 
Beyond  Alzheimer’s  disease,  the  underlying  framework 
can be extended to other neurodegenerative conditions and 
complex medical imaging challenges, advancing the field of 
AI-driven healthcare diagnostics.
